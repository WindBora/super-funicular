\begin{abstract}
We present a full-wave numerical study of plane-wave E-wave (TM)
scattering by two-dimensional open sinusoidal perfectly-electric-conductor
(PEC) strips.
The singular integral equation (SIE) for the induced surface current is
discretized by the method of discrete singularities (MDS), a Nystrom-type
scheme that exploits Chebyshev quadrature and analytically extracted
inverse-square-root edge singularities to achieve spectrally accurate
solutions at modest orders ($N=200$).
A flat-strip benchmark confirms correctness: boundary residuals reach
machine precision (${\sim}10^{-15}$) and the total scattering width
converges monotonically with $N$.
For sinusoidal corrugation the far-field develops lobes at
$\varphi_m=\arccos(m\nu)$, matching the Floquet grating prediction
for integer order $m$ and spatial frequency $\nu$.
A parametric study over $A\in[0.5,\,2.5]\,\lambda$ and
$\nu\in[0.10,\,0.30]\,\lambda^{-1}$ shows that $A$ controls the
blaze strength (energy fraction in off-specular orders) while $\nu$
sets their angular positions.
Total scattering width varies by less than $0.5\%$ across all cases,
confirming purely angular redistribution of the scattered power.
\end{abstract}

method of discrete singularities, sinusoidal open strip, plane-wave

\section{Introduction}

Corrugated metallic surfaces are ubiquitous in microwave engineering.
The sinusoidal open strip is a particularly compact example: it serves
as a meander-line delay element~\cite{wadell1991}, slow-wave resonator,
and unit cell of high-temperature superconducting (HTS) bandpass
filters~\cite{lancaster1997,pozar2011}.
At microwave frequencies, HTS films satisfy $k\lambda_L\lesssim10^{-4}$
(London penetration depth $\lambda_L\!\sim\!100$\,nm), so the PEC
boundary condition is an excellent approximation.

When a plane wave is incident on such a strip, the corrugated surface
couples the specularly reflected field into additional propagating orders
at angles set by the sinusoidal period.
An infinite periodic grating is described analytically by Floquet
theory~\cite{collin1991,balanis2012}, but a finite strip with few periods
has significant edge diffraction that demands a full-wave treatment.

Among rigorous 2-D methods for open-arc scattering, the
\emph{method of discrete singularities}
(MDS)~\cite{nosich2007,gandel2011} combines analytical edge-singularity
extraction with a spectrally accurate Chebyshev Nystrom scheme.
The $N\!\times\!N$ dense linear system is solved exactly by Gaussian
elimination, and boundary residuals reach machine-precision level.
MDS was validated for parabolic multireflector antennas with
complex-source-point (CSP) beams~\cite{nosich2007,nosich_josa2007};
here we apply it to plane-wave excitation and the sinusoidal strip
geometry, performing the first systematic grating-diffraction study
for this finite corrugated conductor.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

\subsection{Boundary-value problem and SIE}

We seek the scattered E-wave field $U$ when a 2-D PEC arc $\Gamma$
is illuminated by a unit-amplitude plane wave
\begin{equation}
  U_0(\mathbf{r})
  = e^{-ik(x\cos\beta + y\sin\beta)},
  \label{eq:planewave}
\end{equation}
with $k=2\pi/\lambda$, all lengths in wavelengths ($\lambda=1$, $k=2\pi$),
and $\beta=\pi/2$ (normal incidence, wave propagating in the $+y$
direction).
The scattered field satisfies the Helmholtz equation outside $\Gamma$,
the Sommerfeld radiation condition, local energy finiteness at arc
endpoints, and the Dirichlet condition $U|_\Gamma=-U_0|_\Gamma$.

The single-layer representation of $U$ leads, after parameterizing the arc
as $\mathbf{r}(t)=(x(t),y(t))$, $t\in[-1,1]$, and factoring the
inverse-square-root edge singularity into a smooth density $v(t)$
via $j\,|\mathbf{r}'(t)|=v(t)(1-t^2)^{-1/2}$, to the first-kind SIE
\begin{equation}
  \int_{-1}^{1}
  H_0^{(1)}\!\left(kR(t,t_0)\right)v(t)\,
  \frac{dt}{\sqrt{1-t^2}}
  = -U_0(t_0),
  \label{eq:sie}
\end{equation}
where $R(t,t_0)=|\mathbf{r}(t)-\mathbf{r}(t_0)|$.

\subsection{MDS discretization, geometry, and Floquet orders}

Differentiating~\eqref{eq:sie} with respect to~$t_0$ converts the
logarithmic kernel singularity to Cauchy type; a supplementary integral
condition restores the lost constant.
Collocating at $n-1$ second-kind Chebyshev nodes
$\tau_j=\cos(j\pi/n)$, $j=1,\dots,n-1$, yields the MDS
system~\cite{nosich2007}
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
  \!\left[\frac{v(t_i)}{t_i-\tau_j}
    + K(t_i,\tau_j)\,v(t_i)\right]
  = f(\tau_j),
  \label{eq:mds}
\end{equation}
where $t_i=\cos\!\frac{(2i-1)\pi}{2n}$ are first-kind Chebyshev nodes,
$K$ is the smooth part of the differentiated Hankel kernel, and
$f=-dU_0/dt_0$.
The $n\!\times\!n$ system is solved by Gaussian elimination.
The far-field pattern $\Phi_{\rm sc}(\varphi)$ follows from the
discretized single-layer potential asymptotically, and the total
2-D scattering width is
\begin{equation}
  \sigma
  = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}
    \bigl|\Phi_{\rm sc}(\varphi)\bigr|^2\,d\varphi.
  \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}

The sinusoidal strip with $L=10\lambda$ and phase zero is
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_s
  = \!\left\{(x,y):\;
  x = \tfrac{L}{2}\,t,\quad
  y = A\sin\!\left(2\pi\nu x\right),\quad
  t\in[-1,1]
  \right\}\!.
  \label{eq:strip}
\end{equation}
For an infinite grating of period $\Lambda=1/\nu$, normal-incidence
Floquet theory gives propagating forward-hemisphere orders at
\begin{equation}
  \varphi_m = \arccos(m\nu),
  \qquad |m\nu|<1.
  \label{eq:floquet}
\end{equation}
The finite strip ($L\nu$ periods) broadens these into beams centered
at~\eqref{eq:floquet}.
With $N=200$ nodes the worst-case arc length ($A=2.5\lambda$,
$\nu=0.30$: $\approx\!48\lambda$) provides ${\approx}4.2$ nodes per
wavelength, sufficient for spectral accuracy.

\subsection{Flat strip benchmark}

  \label{fig:flatstrip}

Setting $A=0$ gives a flat horizontal strip of length $L=10\lambda$.
Near-field and far-field results for normal incidence are shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:flatstrip}; the boundary residual is
$2.12\!\times\!10^{-15}$ (machine precision).
The far-field pattern exhibits the expected two-lobe structure:
the $90^{	rc}$ lobe corresponds to forward diffraction (shadow region),
and the $270^{	rc}$ lobe to backward specular reflection, with weaker
edge-diffraction contributions at $0^{	rc}$ and $180^{	rc}$.

Table~\ref{tab:convergence} shows N-convergence of $\sigma$.
The sequence is monotone and the residual stays below
$3\!\times\!10^{-15}$ throughout, confirming that the inverse-square-root
edge singularity is correctly accounted for and the discrete system is
solved to machine precision.

\subsection{Sinusoidal strip: Floquet-order identification}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig2_sinusoidal.png}
  \caption{%
    Sinusoidal strip ($A=1.5\lambda$, $\nu=0.20\,\lambda^{-1}$,
    $L=10\lambda$, $N=200$).
    \textbf{(a)}~Total near-field intensity; strip profile in magenta.
    \textbf{(b)}~Normalized far-field amplitude (dB); vertical markers
    show predicted Floquet angles:
    $m=0$ at $90^{	rc}$; $m=\pm1$ at $78.5^{	rc}/101.5^{	rc}$;
    $m=\pm2$ at $66.4^{	rc}/113.6^{	rc}$.%
  }
  \label{fig:sinusoidal}
\end{figure*}

For $A=1.5\lambda$, $\nu=0.20\,\lambda^{-1}$ ($L\nu=2$ periods)
the boundary residual is $2.92\!\times\!10^{-15}$.
In the near-field [Fig.~\ref{fig:sinusoidal}(a)], the corrugation
disrupts the regular standing-wave pattern of the flat strip, producing
complex interference structures above and below the arc.

The far-field [Fig.~\ref{fig:sinusoidal}(b)] reveals the key result:
beyond the specular lobes at $90^{	rc}$ and $270^{	rc}$, pronounced peaks appear
at $78.5^{	rc}$, $101.5^{	rc}$, $66.4^{	rc}$, and $113.6^{	rc}$, matching the
Floquet prediction~\eqref{eq:floquet} for $m=\pm1$ and $m=\pm2$
with $\nu=0.20\,\lambda^{-1}$.
The backward hemisphere shows a mirror structure.
Although the finite strip ($L\nu=2$ periods) broadens each lobe,
the peak positions unambiguously confirm the Floquet angles.

\subsection{Parametric pattern evolution}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig3_sweep.png}
  \caption{%
    Forward-hemisphere ($0^{	rc}$--$180^{	rc}$) normalized far-field amplitude (dB)
    illustrating grating-order development.
    \textbf{(a)}~Fixed $\nu=0.20\,\lambda^{-1}$, $A$ varied from
    $0$ to $2.5\lambda$; dotted verticals mark $m=\pm1$ Floquet angles
    ($78.5^{	rc}$ and $101.5^{	rc}$).
    \textbf{(b)}~Fixed $A=1.5\lambda$, $\nu$ varied; dotted verticals
    mark the $m=+1$ angle for each $\nu$
    ($84.3^{	rc}\to72.5^{	rc}$ as $\nu$ increases).%
  }
  \label{fig:sweep}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:sweep} summarizes two parametric sweeps over the
forward hemisphere ($0^{	rc}$--$180^{	rc}$).

\emph{Amplitude dependence} [Fig.~\ref{fig:sweep}(a)]:
For the flat strip ($A=0$) a single dominant lobe sits at $90^{	rc}$.
As $A$ increases at fixed $\nu=0.20\,\lambda^{-1}$, lobes grow
monotonically at the $m=\pm1$ Floquet angles ($78.5^{	rc}$, $101.5^{	rc}$),
while the specular peak broadens.
At $A=2.5\lambda$ the first-order lobes are within a few decibels of
the specular peak, indicating substantial power transfer to off-normal
directions — the finite-strip analog of diffraction-grating blaze.

\emph{Frequency dependence} [Fig.~\ref{fig:sweep}(b)]:
With $A=1.5\lambda$ fixed, increasing $\nu$ shifts the $m=+1$ lobe from
$84.3^{	rc}$ ($\nu=0.10$) to $72.5^{	rc}$ ($\nu=0.30$) following~\eqref{eq:floquet}
exactly.
At $\nu=0.25$--$0.30$ the $m=\pm2$ lobes enter the visible range.
Across all 25 configurations the total scattering width $\sigma$ varies
by less than $0.2\,\lambda$ (${\approx}0.5\%$), confirming that the
sinusoidal corrugation redistributes rather than increases the total
scattered power.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The method of discrete singularities was applied to E-wave plane-wave
scattering by sinusoidal open PEC strips.
The MDS solver was validated on a flat-strip benchmark: boundary residuals
reached machine precision and $\sigma$ converged monotonically with $N$.
For the sinusoidal strip, far-field lobes at the Floquet angles
$\varphi_m=\arccos(m\nu)$ were confirmed numerically for the first time
in the finite-strip setting.
The parametric study identifies two independent design knobs: corrugation
amplitude $A$ controls the blaze strength (fraction of power in
off-specular orders), and spatial frequency $\nu$ sets the grating-order
angles.
Total scattering width remains practically constant (${\lesssim}0.5\%$),
confirming purely angular redistribution.
For superconducting meander-line devices these findings imply that the
corrugation profile controls the far-field scattering signature without
altering the total radiated power, enabling shape-based crosstalk
management in densely integrated microwave circuits.
Planned extensions include H-wave polarization, oblique incidence, and
impedance boundary conditions for resistive or finite-conductivity layers.
